# Abstract

Hierarchical taxonomic prompts are central to recent biological vision-language models such as BioCLIP and BioCLIP2, but their mechanism remains unclear. They may provide additional textual information, or they may organize an embedding space that already contains latent biological structure.
We investigate this distinction with a diagnostic protocol combining flat-versus-hierarchical prompt comparisons, embedding-geometry metrics, rank-resolved taxonomy probes, random-taxonomy controls, and counterfactual prompt ablations. Across BioCLIP2 and OpenCLIP experiments on bird and rare-species datasets, hierarchical prompts do not consistently improve species-level compactness. Instead, they improve organization at higher taxonomic ranks, while ablations show that preserving hierarchical structure matters more than preserving lexical content.
These results suggest that hierarchical prompts are better understood as geometric organizers of pre-existing taxonomic structure than as purely textual information channels. We discuss implications for biological representation learning and outline remaining limitations.
