# 1. Introduction

Fine-grained biological recognition remains a challenging setting for vision-language models. Biological categories are numerous, visually subtle, and highly imbalanced: closely related species may differ only by small morphological cues, while many taxa are represented by only a small number of images. General-purpose CLIP-style models often struggle in this regime, motivating the development of biology-specific vision-language models such as BioCLIP and BioCLIP2 \cite{stevens2024bioclip,gu2025bioclip2}. A central design choice in these models is the use of hierarchical taxonomic prompts, where an image is paired not only with a species name but with a full Linnaean path, such as ``Animalia Chordata Aves Passeriformes Passeridae Passer Passer domesticus.'' This supervision has been associated with strong gains in species recognition and with embedding spaces that appear to reflect ecological and functional structure.

However, the mechanism behind this effect is still unclear. One explanation is that hierarchical prompts act as an additional textual information channel: higher-rank tokens such as family, order, or class provide useful semantic context that improves image-text alignment. A second explanation is that such prompts act primarily as geometric organizers: the visual embedding space may already contain latent taxonomic structure, and hierarchical text supervision may reorganize or amplify this structure rather than inject new biological information. Standard downstream accuracy does not distinguish these accounts, because both can produce similar improvements in classification performance.

This paper studies that distinction through a diagnostic framework for hierarchical prompting in biological vision-language models. We compare flat species-only prompts with hierarchical taxonomic prompts using embedding-geometry metrics, rank-resolved taxonomy probes, random-taxonomy controls, and counterfactual prompt ablations. The ablations are designed to separate lexical content from hierarchical structure: some conditions preserve the taxonomic structure while replacing meaningful words, while others preserve vocabulary but disrupt the hierarchy. This allows us to ask whether the effect of hierarchical prompting depends more on what the prompt says or on how the prompt is structurally organized.

We evaluate this framework in three settings. First, we test BioCLIP2 on CUB-200-2011, a fine-grained bird dataset where all species share the same high-level class. Second, we run the same protocol with OpenCLIP ViT-L/14 as a domain-agnostic baseline. Third, we evaluate BioCLIP2 on a multi-phylum Rare Species dataset, which removes the single-class confound of CUB-200 and tests whether the observed pattern persists across broader taxonomic scope.

Across these settings, we observe a consistent pattern. Hierarchical prompts do not reliably improve species-level compactness; in fact, species-level separation can decrease relative to flat prompts. At higher taxonomic ranks, however, hierarchical prompts provide more organized representations. Counterfactual ablations further show that disrupting hierarchical structure harms the embedding geometry more than removing the original taxonomic words. Finally, image-only taxonomy probes indicate that taxonomic structure is already detectable in the visual embedding space before hierarchical text intervention.

These results are more consistent with the geometric-organizer account than with a purely lexical information-channel account. We do not claim that hierarchical prompts contain no useful textual information, nor that the mechanism is fully resolved. Rather, our results suggest that their effect is better understood as a cross-rank reorganization of an already structured biological embedding space: higher-rank taxonomy becomes more coherent, while species-level compactness may incur a small cost.

Our contributions are as follows:

- We formulate a diagnostic distinction between hierarchical prompts as textual information channels and hierarchical prompts as geometric organizers.
- We introduce a counterfactual prompt-ablation protocol that separates lexical content from hierarchical structure in a domain with ground-truth taxonomy.
- We provide rank-resolved geometric evidence across BioCLIP2, OpenCLIP, CUB-200-2011, and a multi-phylum Rare Species dataset.
- We show that hierarchical prompting is associated with stronger higher-rank taxonomic organization, even when species-level compactness does not improve.
